# The Adjudicator and Phases: The Irony of Automation and the Design of Responsibility

2026-07-05. Non-normative. Related: ADR 0003, `docs/at-tdd/process.md`.

> Translated from [../2026-07-05-rationale-adjudicator-centered-collaboration.md](../2026-07-05-rationale-adjudicator-centered-collaboration.md) as of 2026-07-16. The Japanese original is authoritative.

---

A general consensus that "Human-in-the-loop is necessary" is entirely insufficient to actually operate a project. If where and how humans are positioned is not designed as an architecture, the project will inevitably fall into one of two extremes: either "frantically monitoring every single output of the agent" or "irresponsibly merging all agent output without checking." The former exceeds human cognitive limits and is unsustainable; the latter is an abandonment of software engineering responsibility.

I take a third path: structural collaboration. The human (Adjudicator) explicitly owns the "Decision Points" in the system, and delegates the remaining generation and verification processes to the agent.

The Adjudicator owns phase transition approvals, ADR (Architecture Decision Record) acceptance, Phase 1 test domain reviews, and final decision authority over ambiguous choices. In contrast, the agent owns formulating design intake, context selection, generating code and documentation appropriate to the phase, visualizing assumptions, verifying with deterministic tools, and handing off to the human. The very fact that this responsibility boundary is documented and embedded in the system is the prerequisite for sustainable collaboration (ADR 0003).

## Do Not Make Humans "Constant Monitors": Beyond the Irony of Automation

The most common failure in AI collaboration projects is placing humans in the honorable position of "final authority," only to have them retroactively inspect massive diffs generated by the AI in their entirety. In this scenario, the human is not a creative designer, but merely an exhausted assembly-line worker.

In her classic 1983 paper "Ironies of Automation" ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0005109883900468)), Lisanne Bainbridge pointed out an irony: as automation advances, rather than eliminating the human role, it leaves humans with only the most difficult and complex tasks that the automated system cannot handle. Furthermore, humans relegated to monotonous monitoring find it extremely difficult to accurately grasp the situation and intervene when an anomaly actually occurs.

The exact same irony applies to AI coding. When mass code generation is automated, humans are left with the task of "reading all the contextless code written by AI and guaranteeing its safety"—the task with the highest cognitive load and the highest propensity for error.

Adjudicator-centered collaboration is an architectural countermeasure against this irony of automation. It does not make humans constant monitors; it makes them owners of meaningful decision points. Approving Phase 1 tests. Confirming Phase 2 implementation policies. Deciding whether to adopt an ADR. Halting the process for ambiguous choices regarding providers, DBs, privacy, or schemas. Through this structure, a human's scarce attention is not directed at every generated token, but intensely focused on the points where the meaning of the project irreversibly changes (phase gates).

## The Adjudicator in the Multi-Agent Era: Escalation is Not Approval

The definition of the Adjudicator as the "owner of decision points" does not rely on a structure where a human watches over a single agent. Execution of generation can be divided among multiple LLMs (AutoGen: Wu et al., [arXiv:2308.08155](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08155)), and cascading architectures—where only highly uncertain questions are delegated to strong reasoning models—have been proven to drastically reduce costs while maintaining accuracy (Yue et al., [arXiv:2310.03094](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03094)). Anthropic itself reported operational success outperforming single-model setups by using an orchestrator-worker configuration, placing a strong model as the lead and lighter models as parallel workers ([How we built our multi-agent research system](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/built-multi-agent-research-system). Vendor internal self-report). As this structure becomes generalized, a tempting reinterpretation emerges: "Isn't the Adjudicator simply the smartest reasoning resource placed at the very top of this hierarchy?"

This reinterpretation is elegant as an information flow, but flawed as a design of responsibility. Consulting a strong model and requesting approval from a human may both look like "upward escalation," but their meanings are entirely different. The former is a means to improve the quality of an answer and can be redone infinitely. The latter is an act that finalizes project responsibility and auditability, leaving a record of approval and binding the human to that decision. No matter how deeply layered the agent team becomes, the Adjudicator does not dissolve into the hierarchy as the "smartest advisor," but continues to hold the gate from outside the hierarchy as the "owner of decisions and responsibility." The moment collaboration is reduced merely to "routing reasoning resources," the locus of responsibility immediately becomes ambiguous again.

## AT-TDD: Verbalizing Behavior and the Approval Pipeline

The development cycle is defined as a hybrid of ATDD (Acceptance Test-Driven Development) and TDD (Test-Driven Development). Acceptance specifications drive failing tests, reviewed tests drive minimal implementation, and refactoring occurs only after Green. While Red / Green / Refactor stems from the basic TDD rhythm explained by Martin Fowler ([martinfowler.com](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/TestDrivenDevelopment.html)), the uniqueness of this template lies in externalizing this discipline not as a "programmer's internal self-control," but as a "physical approval gate from agent to human."

The masterpiece *Continuous Delivery* by Jez Humble and David Farley conceptualized the software release process as a "deployment pipeline"—a series of testing (verification) gates where confidence in the change increases as it passes each gate. The "phase gates" in this template shift this pipeline philosophy far upstream (shift-left) into the very heart of development: "during the AI's reasoning for code generation," rather than just at deployment.

I have no intention of marketing "AT-TDD" as a new industry-standard buzzword. Its notation in the README is purely for honesty. The existence of the gates is more important than the name. The idea of placing acceptance specifications first resonates deeply with the philosophies of Specification by Example and BDD (Behavior-Driven Development). Gojko Adzic's *Specification by Example* ([Official Site](https://gojko.net/books/specification-by-example/)) and Dan North's introduction to BDD ([dannorth.net](https://dannorth.net/introducing-bdd/)) emphasize verbalizing "how the system should behave" through concrete examples rather than how it should be built, creating shared understanding among stakeholders.

In AI collaboration, this "verbalization of behavior" and "shared understanding" are even more critical than in human-to-human development. This is because AI agents unapologetically fill gaps in specifications with guesses. Therefore, acceptance specifications with minimal gaps are required upfront. Requirements syntaxes like EARS ([IEEE Xplore](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/5328509)) are extremely effective tools to physically reduce ambiguity on the input side.

## Stopping Phase Skips: The Sequence of Responsibility

"Going straight from an ambiguous request to production code"—this is an AI agent's most characteristic, and most dangerous, failure mode. To prevent this runaway behavior, the absolute prohibition of phase skipping is the primary bulwark. Green after Red; Refactor after Green. The process physically forbids entering Phase 2 implementation without the Phase 1 tests being reviewed by the Adjudicator.

What is important here is that phases are not merely a "sequence of tasks" but a "sequence of responsibility." In Phase 1, the agent translates expected behavior into test code, but does not yet hold responsibility for the production implementation. The Adjudicator verifies that the translation matches the domain requirements. In Phase 2, the agent takes responsibility for writing the minimal implementation to pass the reviewed tests, but conversely is stripped of the "authority to change the tests." In Phase 3, readability is improved without changing behavior. Precisely because authority and responsibility are clearly separated in each phase, a human can concentrate on one single context per review.

Phase skips often arise from the AI's "good intentions." "I fixed this part too while I was at it," "I slightly adjusted the tests to match the implementation," "I also did some refactoring." AI executes these "while I was at it" tasks rapidly and seamlessly. That is exactly why we prohibit them. Dividing work into small chunks is not an enemy that reduces AI productivity. It is a safety mechanism to preserve human-reviewable units and maintain the velocity of the overall project.

## Attention is a Scarce Resource

Human review should not be directed at every token. ADR 0003 explicitly states, "Human review is focused on decision points instead of every token of output." Attention must be designed into the system as the most scarce resource in software development. The goal is not to blindly increase the number of gates. The goal is to maintain the quality and weight of the decision points. The risk of turning the process into mere cumbersome paperwork—which ADR 0003 lists as a Negative—must be constantly guarded against during operation.

The Human-AI Interaction guidelines by Amershi et al. ([Microsoft Research](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/guidelines-for-human-ai-interaction/)) emphasize the importance of AI systems showing information to users at appropriate times to enable correction and intervention. The Adjudicator loop in this template is an architectural design that treats the entire development process as a massive Human-AI Interaction system. It explicitly names the points where humans should intervene as phase transitions, ADR acceptance, test reviews, and ambiguity decisions, and automates the rest.

## Increased Capability is No Excuse to Remove Gates

Even if the base capabilities of AI models improve and they become "smarter," the Adjudicator's ownership and phase gates are not abolished. Gates are not a temporary workaround for lack of capability; they are an immutable structure for responsibility and auditability in software. The more an agent can generate autonomously, the greater the damage if it goes rogue, making the design of decision points even more critical.

Strong models can generate more persuasive explanations and broader changes at once. While extremely convenient, this is also a destructive power that can instantly permeate a codebase with false premises. In the era of weak models, the greatest danger was "what AI couldn't do." In the era of strong models, the greatest danger is "what AI can do (incorrectly)." The role of the Adjudicator is not to blindly stop the AI out of suspicion of its capabilities, but to control its immense power so that it does not cross the boundaries of the project's responsibility structure.

This document itself does not add new gates to the operational template. If the philosophy described here needs to advance to concrete rules, it must be separately elevated to an ADR or collaboration document ([Normative vs. Reading Documents](./2026-07-06-rationale-normative-vs-reading-documents.md)). Guarding that line is also an important part of Adjudicator-centered collaboration.
